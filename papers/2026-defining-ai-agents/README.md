## Abstract

The scalability and governance challenges of AI agents trace back to a
fundamental architectural deficit: the absence of an explicit, semantically
expressive, and structurally adaptive abstraction layer. The central limitation
of existing management and computer science approaches is not the absence of
semantic, structural, organizational, or formal methods individually, but the
lack of a common abstraction layer through which these dimensions can be unified.
This paper introduces an architectural framework utilizing explicit,
machine-readable ontological and topological representations for AI agents to
resolve this gap. Ontology specifies the semantic categories and relations
constituting an AI system's representational world, while topology models the
structural organization and connectivity through which these ontological elements
interact. Leveraging the formalisms of category theory, mereotopology, and graph
theory, we formally define the framework and prove its five core properties:
composability, interoperability, provenance preservation, auditability, and
formal verifiability. Finally, we demonstrate that the implications of this
framework extend far beyond pure technical representation, offering critical
paradigm shifts for complex adaptive systems, AI governance, epistemic
transparency, organizational theory, game theory, and composable AI ecosystems.
